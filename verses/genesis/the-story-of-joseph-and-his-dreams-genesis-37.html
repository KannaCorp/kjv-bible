Joseph's Dreams: Unpacking Genesis 37's Tale of Prophecy and Family Strife

The story of Joseph and his dreams, as recounted in Genesis chapter 37, stands as a foundational narrative within the Old Testament. It introduces us to a young man favored by his father, Jacob, whose vivid prophetic dreams and a special coat spark a devastating wave of jealousy among his eleven brothers. This pivotal chapter sets the stage for a dramatic saga of betrayal, suffering, and ultimately, God's sovereign plan unfolding through unexpected turns, laying the groundwork for Israel's journey into Egypt.

Genesis 37 opens by introducing Joseph, the eleventh son of Jacob and the first son of his beloved wife, Rachel. Jacob’s deep affection for Joseph was evident, particularly in his gift of a 'coat of many colours' (Genesis 37:3). This distinctive garment, often interpreted as a 'long coat with sleeves' or a royal robe, signified Joseph’s elevated status and Jacob's intention to bestow special authority upon him, perhaps even that of the firstborn. However, this open display of favoritism deeply offended his older brothers, who already resented Joseph, perceiving him as a tattletale for bringing their evil reports to their father.

Further fueling the brothers’ animosity were two vivid dreams Joseph recounted. In the first dream, he and his brothers were binding sheaves in the field, and suddenly Joseph's sheaf stood upright, while his brothers' sheaves gathered around and bowed down to it (Genesis 37:7). His second dream was even more explicit: the sun, the moon, and eleven stars bowed down to him (Genesis 37:9). These dreams, clearly prophetic of Joseph’s future authority over his family, were met with utter contempt and indignation. His brothers exclaimed, 'Shalt thou indeed reign over us? or shalt thou indeed have dominion over us?' (Genesis 37:8), and their hatred intensified.

The climax of Genesis 37 details the brothers’ malicious plot. While Joseph was sent by Jacob to check on them as they tended their flocks in Dothan, they conspired to kill him. Reuben, the eldest, intervened, suggesting they throw Joseph into a dry pit instead, secretly planning to rescue him later. However, while Reuben was absent, the other brothers, seeing a caravan of Ishmeelites heading to Egypt, decided to sell Joseph into slavery for twenty pieces of silver (Genesis 37:28). They then dipped Joseph’s magnificent coat in goat’s blood and presented it to Jacob, leading their father to believe a wild beast had devoured his beloved son, plunging him into deep mourning.

This chapter, though filled with human jealousy and betrayal, is crucial for understanding God's overarching providence. Joseph's dreams, though initially a source of strife, were divine revelations of God's future plans. Even through the cruel actions of his brothers, God was orchestrating events that would ultimately preserve Jacob's family and pave the way for the formation of the nation of Israel. Genesis 37 introduces the themes of divine sovereignty, human sin, and the mysterious ways God uses difficult circumstances to fulfill His purposes, setting the stage for Joseph's incredible journey from pit to palace.
